## The shift we're betting on

AI has spent its first decade living in software. It writes, it summarizes, it answers questions, it generates images. Useful, but abstract — confined to screens, text, and pixels.

The next decade is different. AI is moving into the physical world — into the buildings, the spaces, the operations where things actually happen. **Physical AI** isn't about smarter chatbots. It's about agents that perceive real-world signals, reason about them in context, and take action that affects the physical environment.

Buildings are where this shift starts. They generate continuous streams of telemetry — temperature, occupancy, cleanliness, security, equipment health — and they require continuous decisions and actions. They're the perfect domain for AI that doesn't just observe but operates.

Merlin is the bet that the right way to build physical AI isn't one giant model trying to do everything, but a coordinated fleet of specialized agents — each one expert in its concern, each one accountable for its outcomes, each one part of a larger intelligence working on the building together.

## The Smart Display — the face of physical AI

Most AI systems are invisible. They run in the background, somewhere in the cloud, making decisions the people they serve never see. Physical AI has to be different. If an intelligence is shaping how a space gets cleaned, how the temperature is managed, how a problem gets reported — the people inside that space should be able to see it.

The Adaptiv Smart Display is where Merlin's intelligence becomes visible. It's the screen on the wall — at eye level, in the corridor, in the restroom, in the lobby — that turns abstract backend reasoning into something occupants can see, touch, and trust.

What it shows depends on the space. In a restroom: when it was last cleaned and by whom, the current air quality, the temperature in the room. In a corridor or lobby: occupancy patterns, environmental conditions, status of the spaces around it. Everywhere: a single tap to request service, report a problem, or send feedback to the team responsible.

This solves the hardest problem in AI adoption — making the technology feel human and approachable rather than distant and automated. The screen on the wall is the handshake between the building and the people inside it.

### Sensor, gateway, and interface — all at once

The Smart Display is uniquely powerful because it plays three roles simultaneously, where every other device in the built environment plays only one.

- **It is a sensor.** Seven embedded sensors capture environmental conditions, register occupant feedback, and verify staff identity via NFC for service-confirmation flows.
- **It is a gateway.** Each Smart Display aggregates data from nearby wireless peripheral sensors and relays it to the cloud — no SIM card, no separate gateway hardware, no additional network infrastructure.
- **It is an interface.** Merlin's intelligence is presented back to occupants in real time, and occupants interact with it directly through the display.

A hidden sensor can measure but cannot communicate. A traditional screen can display but cannot sense. The Smart Display does both — and because it runs on battery and e-ink, it installs anywhere without power infrastructure. Every wall in every corridor, restroom, lobby, and stairwell becomes an intelligent node in a building-wide AI network.

## The sensor library — eyes and ears at every point

The Smart Display is the hub. Around it, Adaptiv has designed and manufactures a growing library of independent wireless sensors that extend the platform's reach to every point in a managed space.

Each sensor operates entirely on battery power, communicates with the nearest Smart Display over short-range wireless protocols, and relies on the display as its sole gateway to the cloud. No SIM cards. No wiring. No separate network infrastructure. The Smart Display and its peripheral sensors form a mesh — the display is the hub, the sensors are the nodes, and together they give Merlin eyes and ears at every location that matters.

### What's in the library today

- **People counters** — measure foot traffic at entrances and corridors.
- **Dispenser level monitors** — track soap, paper towel, and sanitizer fill levels in real time.
- **Leak detectors** — alert within seconds of water presence.
- **Door and stall occupancy sensors** — detect open and closed states.
- **Bin level sensors** — measure waste container fill.
- **Radar sensing devices** — privacy-safe millimetre-wave occupancy detection.
- **Machine vision devices** — visual anomaly detection in demanding environments.
- **Smart lighting** — illumination combined with embedded occupancy and energy sensing.

Each sensor was designed to address a specific gap the Smart Display's embedded array cannot cover from its fixed wall position — the dispenser across the room, the stall at the end of the corridor, the entrance ten metres away.

### How the library grows

Adaptiv continuously evaluates new sensing technologies to add to the library. Three criteria guide every addition:

1. **Does the sensor capture a signal Merlin cannot already infer from existing data?**
2. **Does it extend coverage to a location or modality the Smart Display cannot reach?**
3. **Is the hardware and deployment cost justified by the operational intelligence it provides?**

The discipline matters. The ecosystem grows with intention rather than proliferation — each addition strengthens Merlin's model of the building rather than adding noise.

For customers, the implication is straightforward: the platform you deploy today becomes more capable over time as new sensor types are added to the library, without any change required to the Smart Display infrastructure already on your walls.

## What "specialized agent" means, and why it matters

A general-purpose AI doesn't know what good cleaning looks like in a pharmacy. It doesn't know the regulatory constraints on cold-chain storage. It doesn't know when a comfort-band drift in HVAC is normal seasonal variation versus a failing compressor. It can be told, but it doesn't carry the domain.

A specialized agent does.

Specialized agents are scoped tightly to a single operational concern. Each one carries the domain knowledge, the regulatory constraints, the vertical-specific judgment, and the action vocabulary of its area. The HVAC agent thinks about HVAC. The cleaning agent thinks about cleaning. The cold-chain agent thinks about cold-chain. They don't try to be each other.

This matters for three reasons:

**Specialization beats generality where stakes are real.** A pharmacy refrigeration deviation is a regulatory event, not a tip. An HVAC anomaly in a hospital is a patient-safety question, not a maintenance ticket. The judgment required is domain-specific. Specialized agents bring that judgment by design.

**Specialized agents can be measured and improved one at a time.** When the cleaning agent gets better, cleaning operations get better — without disturbing HVAC, cold-chain, or anything else. When a new operational concern matters enough to merit its own intelligence, a new agent is trained for it and added to the library. The whole system gets smarter agent by agent, domain by domain, rather than as one big bet that has to be right about everything at once.

**Specialization mirrors how the world actually organizes expertise.** Nobody hires one person to do plumbing, refrigeration, fire safety, and access control. You hire specialists, and they coordinate. Merlin's agent fleet mirrors that structure — specialists, coordinating.

## Inside an agent's loop

Every specialized agent in Merlin runs the same fundamental loop, continuously. The shape of it is what makes physical AI different from the AI that has come before.

### Sense

The agent reads the world. Telemetry from sensors, status from devices, recent events, the building's schedule, the conditions of the spaces it cares about. Not a snapshot — a continuous stream.

### Reason

The agent applies its domain knowledge to what it's reading. Is this normal? Is this drifting? Is this an emergency? It carries the vertical's constraints — what's acceptable in a warehouse is not acceptable in a clinic — and the building's specific operating context.

### Decide

The agent forms an opinion about what should happen. Sometimes that's "nothing, keep watching." Sometimes it's "alert a human." Sometimes it's "dispatch the contractor responsible for this." Sometimes it's "auto-execute, log it, move on."

### Act

The agent does the thing. Files the work order. Notifies the responsible party. Drafts the message. Updates the schedule. Triggers the device. The action is real and recorded.

### Measure

The agent watches what happens next. Did the contractor respond? Did the issue resolve? How long did it take? Was the action effective? The outcome feeds back into the agent's understanding of its domain — and into the accountability of the people and vendors who executed.

This loop — sense, reason, decide, act, measure — is what separates physical AI from passive monitoring. A dashboard tells you something is wrong. A specialized agent does something about it, and then verifies the result.

## A co-worker that sees it coming

The most valuable thing a great colleague does isn't reacting fast — it's seeing the problem before it arrives. Because Merlin reads the building continuously, it doesn't just report the present; it anticipates the future, and acts while there's still time to change the outcome.

- **Forecast.** A rolling read on what's coming across the building — conditions, load, demand, and the spaces about to need attention — so the day is planned around what's ahead, not what already happened.
- **Predictive maintenance.** Equipment rarely fails without warning. Merlin catches the failure _signature_ — the compressor drifting, the pump vibrating past baseline — and schedules the fix while it's still cheap, before it takes a floor or a freezer offline.
- **Savings and energy.** Consumption is tracked against budget in real time, and the leaks — a zone running hot, a system cycling through an empty building — are surfaced before the bill lands.
- **Wellbeing.** Air quality, comfort, and cleanliness are watched as trajectories, not snapshots, so a space is corrected before it becomes unpleasant or unhealthy to occupy.
- **Risk, ahead of time.** SLA breaches, compliance drift, and safety thresholds are flagged on approach — while the clock still favors a save — not logged after the fact.

Every one of these is the same closed loop pointed at the future: sense the trend, reason about where it leads, decide whether to act now, act, and measure. A dashboard reports yesterday. A co-worker prepares you for tomorrow.

Rules change, regulations stack up, certifications demand evidence, and occupant wellbeing is now a standard in its own right. When the inspector or the certifier arrives, the evidence is already there — and the building's wellbeing and sustainability claims are backed by data, not declarations.

Your co-worker doesn't just keep the building running. It keeps it provably in line with everything it's held to.

## Why buildings are where physical AI starts

Buildings are an ideal first domain for physical AI for reasons that are easy to underestimate.

**They're instrumented.** Modern buildings generate enormous volumes of continuous telemetry. Sensors are cheap, ubiquitous, and getting more so. The signal exists; it just needs to be reasoned over.

**The decisions are bounded and repetitive.** "Dispatch a cleaning contractor" is a well-defined action with well-defined success criteria. The space of reasonable agent decisions is finite and learnable. This is unlike, say, open-ended creative work.

**The stakes are real but recoverable.** A bad cleaning dispatch is annoying. A missed temperature alert in a pharmacy is a compliance event. The consequences range from low-stakes to high-stakes, which means agents can be trusted with progressively more authority as they prove themselves.

**The accountability chain is concrete.** Buildings involve people, contractors, vendors, owners — each with defined responsibilities. The agent fleet doesn't replace those people; it organizes the information flow between them and ensures every action is tracked to outcome.

**The problem matters.** Buildings consume a substantial fraction of global energy. They host the bulk of indoor human activity. Better-run buildings mean lower emissions, better health outcomes, safer spaces, and more productive work. Solving operations for the physical world isn't a niche — it's foundational infrastructure.

## Where this technology goes

Buildings are the first domain. They aren't the last.

The same architecture — sensors generate telemetry, specialized agents reason over it, agents act in coordination with humans and contractors, every action is measured — generalizes to any physical-world operation. Just as importantly, **so does the hardware.** The Smart Display that lives at eye level in a corridor or restroom belongs equally on a refinery floor, beside a cold-storage door, at a hospital nurse station, in a data center aisle, or in a metro station concourse. Battery and e-ink mean no power infrastructure is required. The sensor mesh means peripheral signals come along with no additional wiring. The same physical layer that proves itself in office and retail spaces today installs into adjacent verticals without re-engineering. What changes between domains isn't the device — it's which specialized agent reasons over its signals.

Some near-adjacent applications:

### Cold-chain logistics

The same temperature-compliance intelligence that protects a clinical refrigerator extends across warehouses, distribution centers, transport fleets, and last-mile delivery. The Smart Display goes on the freezer door; leak detectors, door-state sensors, and dedicated temperature probes round out the mesh. End-to-end traceability for pharma, food, biologics, and reagents — with the human-facing screen exactly where staff need it.

### Industrial and manufacturing

Production-floor cleanliness, machine telemetry, safety incident response, regulatory compliance. Machine-vision devices catch visual anomalies in demanding environments. The Smart Display becomes the shift handover point, the safety reporting interface, and the operator's window into the agent fleet's work — all in one place on the wall.

### Data centers and critical infrastructure

Power, cooling, security, and environmental compliance at rack-level granularity. Radar occupancy sensors track who's in which aisle; leak detectors guard cooling infrastructure; Smart Displays at zone entrances surface the status of the racks behind them. Specialized agents for each concern, coordinating to keep critical systems running.

### Hospitality and care environments

Hotels, senior living facilities, and care homes — where the operational concerns are guest experience, housekeeping accountability, environmental compliance, and resident safety. Each gets its own specialized agent.

### Schools, universities, and public buildings

Campus-wide operations across many spaces. Occupancy-driven energy, classroom utilization, environmental health. Operations intelligence at the scale of an institution.

### Retail and multi-site operations

Operational consistency across stores. Cleanliness standards, equipment reliability, customer-experience signals. The agent fleet keeps the brand standard real across every location.

### Transportation hubs and transit

Stations, terminals, ports — high-volume public spaces with continuous operational demands. Cleaning, safety, accessibility, environmental quality. All natural domains for specialized agents.

### Smart cities and public infrastructure

Beyond single buildings, into the operational fabric of districts and cities. Streetlights, public restrooms, transit stops, parks, civic buildings. Physical AI at municipal scale.

The pattern in every case is the same. Sensors generate signal. Specialized agents reason in their domain. Humans and vendors execute. The system measures the outcome and learns from it. Whether the physical environment is a warehouse, a hospital, a refinery, or a metro station, the architecture works because the loop is the same — and the hardware that runs the loop is the same too. A Smart Display, a mesh of wireless sensors, a fleet of specialized agents, a chat layer for the people running the operation. One platform, many environments, one foundation underneath.
